## Premise

**A good robot goes anywhere. Good technology belongs to everyone.** Every design decision behind AntBot traces back to these two lines. From where each sensor sits to how the wheels turn to how much of the code we share. If it didn't serve the premise, it didn't ship.

## Last Mile

The last mile is the shortest stretch in logistics, and the hardest. Campus walkways, apartment blocks, underground garages. Tight alleys, curbs, ramps, pedestrians who don't look up. No lane markings. No traffic lights. No fixed route.

What a robot needs to survive here boils down to one thing. Anywhere. Any direction. Right now. Every piece of AntBot's hardware exists to make that sentence real.

## Swerve Drive

Each of AntBot's four wheels steers on its own. That gives the robot omnidirectional movement, zero-radius turns, and sideways travel. Where a differential-drive robot would need a three-point turn to get out, a swerve drive slips through in one move.

Last-mile throws endless exceptions. Dead ends, surprise obstacles, tight elevator lobbies. Rather than patching them one by one, a single drivetrain absorbs them all.

## Sensor Architecture

Going anywhere means seeing everything. AntBot's sensor layout is built to leave no gap.

A roof-mounted 3D LiDAR sweeps 360 degrees and maps the space. Two 2D LiDARs at the front and rear catch what sits close to the ground: curbs, planters, low posts. An RGB-D camera reads depth so the robot knows how far and what shape. Four mono cameras cover the rest, eliminating blind spots.

Each sensor picks up where another falls short. Cameras supply texture that LiDAR cannot. LiDAR's active pulses see through darkness that cameras cannot. This is not a stack of parts. It is one perceptual system.

## Open Source

Technology left closed is technology left unfinished. AntBot ships its full software stack: controllers, sensor drivers, navigation and all.

Outdoor autonomy generates edge cases without end. Wet manhole covers, drainage grates, headlights glaring at night. Answers to problems like these don't come from a conference room. They come from the field, from the people who run the robot day after day.

We trust a community's experience over any single team's ability. Opening the code is our commitment to that experience, and our invitation to yours.
